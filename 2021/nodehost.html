CASE STUDY

A branding, web, and UI/UX design update for a Philadelphia-based cryptocurrency startup.

OVERVIEW

As a newcomer to the crypto-currency scene, it was imperative that Nodehost quickly establish itself as credible, reliable, and secure, whether for serious investors and traders, or casual crypto-currency enthusiasts. The competition across the industry was stiff. Many other companies had long-term funding, established brand value, and profitable partnerships. While Nodehost had rock-solid engineering, competitive pricing and an expanding feature set, they were lacking a user-friendly experience, consistent branding, and overall visual polish.

I was brought on as the sole designer on the project in late 2018 to solve these problems, working closely with the founders, a back-end engineer, and a marketing strategist, to push the evolution of the brand, logo, and social media presence, and redesign their website with an improved UX design and future-proof front-end codebase.

BRANDING

My work began by expanding and streamlining the existing branding elements, adding more supporting shades and tints to the color palette, establishing some global text styles using Barlow, and exploring visual elements to use as supportive and decorative elements. Unifying the brand behind these simple guidelines expanded the flexibility of visuals with greater contrast, hierarchy and visual interest while maintaining consistency.

Along with these changes, a redesign of the logo (as seen above) brought some much needed dynamism and visual movement to the brand, allowing us to visually represent the future-facing and ever-changing nature of the industry, as well as subtly show the role of Nodehost itself, as a service provider that helped crypto investors connect nodes within a network.

WEBSITE

After an internal review of the existing website and discussion with the rest of the team identifying which pages needed content updates, reorganization, or replacement, I started work on redesigning the website. Starting with some rough wireframes, I updated the layout of each page, focusing on content hierarchy, user flow, and avoiding visual repetition. Due to the technical content on many of the pages, I brought in some simple icons and spent significant time focusing on typographical hierarchy and balancing white spaces to ensure that these pages felt easy to read, important information was highlighted correctly and the technical jargon was appropriately spaced.

These pages were the main source of information about Nodehost and were the primary landing points from social media interactions and web banner ad links. It was an important business goal to use these pages to both establish a strong branding presence and strike a balance in tone between being friendly to new users and showing expertise to existing investors and experts.

DASHBOARD

Towards the end of my time with Nodehost, I was also able to start exploring how the new branding and web guidelines would be implemented into the webapp platform itself, and test the existing guidelines against a new type of content and interface. Some initial feedback on these early explorations revealed that the heavy reliance on the primary purple, which was effective branding on the landing pages, was less effective on the platform itself as it created a visual hierarchy that was less clear to the user. Bringing in a new “action” color to highlight buttons, links and other actions would have been the next step on improving these mock-ups.
